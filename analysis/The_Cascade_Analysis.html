Section 01

The Moment Nobody Has Planned For

I want to start with a scenario I have thought about seriously — because in the current threat environment, I believe every security professional should. It is a Tuesday morning. My phone shows no signal. I assume it is a temporary outage — a tower issue, a software glitch. I wait. Five minutes pass. Ten. I try to connect to WiFi and the router blinks but produces nothing. I turn on the television and get a static screen on every channel. I step outside. My neighbor is standing in her driveway, phone in hand, the same confusion on her face.

My children are home. My mother lives in New York City — she does not own a car, she depends entirely on public transportation, and her building has no landline. I have no way to reach her. I do not know whether to stay, to drive toward her, or to wait for instructions that are not coming — because every system designed to deliver those instructions has gone silent simultaneously.

This is not a disaster movie premise. It is the logical outcome of a coordinated attack on three infrastructure systems that the United States has spent the last two decades making faster, more connected, and more interdependent — while stripping away every analog backup that once provided resilience. The attack does not require nuclear weapons. It does not require boots on the ground. It requires pre-positioned access, a coordinated trigger, and the patience to wait for the right moment. All three of those conditions already exist.

Analytical Framing: This analysis is not a prediction of imminent catastrophe. It is a professional assessment of documented vulnerabilities, confirmed pre-positioning by nation-state actors, and the structural gaps in civilian preparedness that transform a technical attack into a societal crisis. The goal is not alarm — it is the kind of clear-eyed risk mapping that policymakers, defense contractors, and security professionals must undertake before the event, not after. Every vulnerability described in this report is publicly confirmed. Every gap is currently unfixed. Every recommendation is actionable today.

Section 02

The Strategic Doctrine — Why America Defeats Itself

In studying how a sophisticated adversary would approach a conflict with the United States, one insight stands above all others: a direct military confrontation is the least efficient path to victory. The United States maintains the most powerful conventional and nuclear military force in human history. No nation can defeat it on a traditional battlefield in any reasonable timeframe.

But the United States has a different kind of vulnerability — one that has grown larger every year as the country became more technologically dependent and less resilient. That vulnerability is the civilian population's total dependence on infrastructure systems that were never designed to withstand coordinated attack, and the near-complete absence of any plan for what ordinary people should do when those systems fail simultaneously.

The Attacker's Calculus

A sophisticated nation-state adversary — China, Russia, or a proxy coalition — does not need to defeat the American military. It needs to make the American military unable to function by ensuring that everything the military depends on for domestic stability has collapsed. When police and National Guard are overwhelmed managing civilian unrest driven by food scarcity and communications blackout, they are not available to respond to the external threat. When military logistics depend on civilian communications and power infrastructure, those logistics degrade in parallel with the civilian systems.

Core Strategic Assessment: The most effective attack on a superpower is not destruction of its military capacity — it is destruction of the civilian coordination infrastructure that allows a society to function, organize, and respond. An already divided population does not need to be pushed into chaos. It needs only to lose the thin infrastructure of communication and resource distribution that currently holds it together. The attacker wins not by defeating America but by making America defeat itself.

Pre-Positioning: The War That Is Already Underway

This analysis does not describe a hypothetical future threat. The pre-positioning phase of this scenario is confirmed and active. Volt Typhoon — a Chinese state-sponsored threat actor — has maintained persistent access inside US power grid infrastructure, water systems, communications networks, and transportation systems since at least 2021, with the FBI confirming some footholds will never be found. Russia's Sandworm unit has demonstrated destructive capability against power grid infrastructure in Ukraine. Iranian-linked actors executed a mass wipe of 200,000 devices across a US medical technology company in March 2026 using only legitimate administrative tools.

The infrastructure access required to execute the cascade scenario described in this report is not theoretical. It exists today, in confirmed form, sitting dormant and waiting for a geopolitical trigger.

Section 03

Phase 1 — Kill the Voice: The Communications Strike

Communications infrastructure is the correct first target in a coordinated attack, and the reasoning is not primarily technical — it is psychological and organizational. When communication fails, three things happen simultaneously that no other single attack can achieve: the population cannot talk to each other, emergency services cannot coordinate, and the government cannot talk to citizens. The result is an information vacuum that is itself a weapon independent of any physical damage.

The Scale of Cellular Dependency

The United States has undergone a fundamental shift in communications infrastructure that has no historical precedent and no adequate emergency plan. Three out of four American adults now live in wireless-only households. For adults aged 25-34, that figure reaches 90%. Renters — who make up a significant portion of urban populations in high-density cities like New York, Chicago, and Los Angeles — are wireless-only at a rate of 85%.

This means that cellular infrastructure is no longer a convenience technology. It is the only emergency communications infrastructure most Americans possess. There is no fallback. When it fails, the silence is total.

The Vulnerability Is Confirmed: Security researchers documented 119 vulnerabilities across LTE and 5G implementations in 2025, every one of which can be used to persistently disrupt all cellular communications — phone calls, messaging, and data — at a city-wide scale. Salt Typhoon, a Chinese state-sponsored actor, has already achieved persistent real-time access to US cellular networks, including the law enforcement wiretapping infrastructure. Security professionals now operate under the explicit assumption that US cellular networks are compromised channels.

The Emergency Alert System: The Last Warning System That Can Be Weaponized

The Emergency Alert System was designed as the mechanism of last resort — the system that allows the President to address the country within ten minutes across all broadcast channels during a national emergency. It is the infrastructure equivalent of a fire alarm for the entire nation. A sophisticated adversary would not simply disable it. They would weaponize it.

EAS equipment across the country runs on aging platforms with documented vulnerabilities, factory-default passwords, and unpatched firmware. Security researchers have publicly demonstrated working exploits capable of issuing fake emergency alerts over TV, radio, and cable networks. The system is administered locally by thousands of independent broadcast stations, creating enormous inconsistency in security posture.

The Weaponized Information Vacuum: The most dangerous aspect of a communications strike is not silence — it is controlled noise. A coordinated attack would not simply cut all signals. It would first flood social media and broadcast channels with contradictory disinformation — fake alerts sending populations in different directions, conflicting reports about the source and nature of the attack, fabricated government statements. By the time the actual infrastructure disruption begins, the population is already fragmented by competing false narratives. People are not just uninformed — they actively disagree about what is happening, who is responsible, and what they should do. That disagreement is more paralyzing than silence.

The Landline Question — A Critical Policy Failure in Progress

The one communications technology that is largely immune to the cyber vectors that threaten cellular infrastructure is the analog copper landline. A true POTS line has no software, no operating system, no remote attack surface. Physically, intercepting it requires access to a specific wire. It cannot be jammed at scale. It does not require external power at the handset. It does not depend on towers or network switching infrastructure that can be taken down by a cyberattack on routing systems.

The United States is currently dismantling this infrastructure deliberately. AT&T announced in December 2024 its intention to shut down virtually all POTS lines by 2029, saving $6 billion annually. The FCC declared POTS an "outmoded legacy service" serving "no beneficial public interest purpose" and removed price caps, allowing carriers to price landlines at $40-70 per month for residential customers to accelerate customer abandonment. The copper infrastructure that took over a century to build is being decommissioned for commercial efficiency.

The Infrastructure Paradox: The copper switching infrastructure already exists. The poles, the wires, the central offices — they are already in place and largely paid for over more than a century of service. The argument for decommissioning is not that the infrastructure is absent but that maintaining it is less profitable than encouraging migration to cellular and VoIP. This is a commercial decision being made with national security consequences that have not been adequately assessed. Decommissioning the most resilient communications infrastructure in the country, at the moment when nation-state adversaries have confirmed access to its cellular replacement, is not a technology upgrade. It is a preparedness failure disguised as modernization.

Section 05

Phase 3 — Freeze the Supply Chain: Transportation and the Food System

Transportation is the third target because it closes the trap that communications failure and power failure have already set. With no communications, populations cannot coordinate distribution. With no power, food cannot be stored or refrigerated. With no transportation, food that exists in the wrong location cannot reach the people who need it. The three failures together create a scarcity condition that no individual component failure could produce alone.

The Three-Day Retail Inventory Reality

The United States food distribution system operates on a just-in-time model that prioritizes efficiency over resilience. The average retail grocery store carries approximately three days of inventory under normal operating conditions. This is not a flaw — it is a deliberate design choice that minimizes warehousing cost and food waste. Under normal conditions, continuous restocking maintains the appearance of abundance. Under disrupted conditions, the shelves are bare within 72 hours.

Aviation: Thousands of Aircraft and a GPS Dependency

On any given day, approximately 45,000 flights operate in US airspace. Every one of them depends on GPS for navigation, and GPS spoofing — the technique of broadcasting fake satellite signals to mislead receivers — has moved from theoretical threat to documented operational reality. Nation-states have demonstrated GPS spoofing capability in conflict zones at a scale of more than 1,500 affected flights per day in the Middle East alone. Similar interference has been detected near major US airports including Dallas and Denver.

Air Traffic Control systems represent a separate and compounding vulnerability. Legacy ATC infrastructure runs on systems that were not designed with cybersecurity in mind, and the transition to modernized NextGen systems has created a hybrid environment where old and new systems interact in ways that introduce additional attack surface. A coordinated attack that spoofs GPS signals while disrupting ATC communications during peak air traffic hours would create a crisis of aviation safety that grounds the entire national air traffic system within minutes.

The Rail and Port Dimension: Rail networks depend on computerized switching systems and communications infrastructure. A communications blackout disrupts train dispatch and creates collision risk that forces immediate network shutdown. Container ports — which handle the majority of US import and export volume — depend on networked crane systems, logistics management software, and communications infrastructure for coordinated operations. A simultaneous disruption of aviation, rail, and port operations does not merely slow supply chains. It stops them. Goods already in transit cannot be delivered. Goods needed for emergency response cannot be moved.

Section 06

The Human Layers — Sleeper Networks and Surveillance Inversion

Sleeper Networks: The Crowbar, Not the Hammer

Conventional analysis of foreign sleeper networks focuses on their role as post-attack amplifiers — physical actors who create additional chaos after an infrastructure attack has begun. This framing misunderstands how sophisticated intelligence operations actually work. Physical human assets operating inside the United States are most valuable not after the attack but before it — as the initial penetration capability that establishes the cyber footholds that make the technical attack possible.

A nation-state mounting a long-term pre-positioning campaign against critical infrastructure needs initial access to systems that are not internet-connected, or whose internet connectivity is tightly controlled. A human asset — a recruited insider with legitimate access, a coerced employee with family abroad, a vendor technician whose company was compromised — can provide credentials, network diagrams, physical access tokens, and operational knowledge that no remote cyber operation can easily obtain. They are the crowbar that opens the door. The cyber operation walks through it.

Confirmed Intelligence Assessment: The Director of the National Counterterrorism Center confirmed to Congress in December 2025 that approximately 18,000 known and suspected individuals with ties to jihadist groups entered the US during the prior administration. Separately, declassified FBI cases confirm longstanding Hezbollah networks with documented sleeper and surveillance capabilities in New York City, Texas, and other major cities — historically focused on financial crimes but with confirmed activation capabilities. US security assessments identify Iran as maintaining a calculated plan to foster internal alliances including sleeper cells, with nearly a dozen Iran-connected plots already thwarted on US soil. Russia's foreign intelligence service SVR has run long-term human intelligence operations inside US critical infrastructure for decades. China's Ministry of State Security is assessed as running the most sophisticated and patient long-term human recruitment operations of any foreign intelligence service.

The post-attack role of physical assets is more targeted than broad destabilization: disrupting the repair and restoration operations that would end the crisis. A power grid that can be restored in two weeks becomes a years-long crisis if the repair crews and restoration infrastructure face physical interference. This is the scenario that transforms a severe but recoverable infrastructure attack into a civilizational-scale event.

The Surveillance Inversion — Every Smart Device Is a Window In

The second human-layer threat is one that most cybersecurity analysis treats as a privacy concern rather than a national security vulnerability. It should be treated as both. The average American home in 2026 contains approximately 22 internet-connected devices. Smart televisions with built-in microphones. Voice-activated speakers — Alexa, Google Home, Siri-enabled devices — that are listening continuously by design. Ring cameras and smart doorbells that stream video to cloud servers. Smart thermostats that record occupancy patterns. Home routers running unpatched firmware from 2019. And baby monitors — devices that are confirmed hackable, with documented cases of strangers speaking directly through them to children in their cribs. If an attacker can speak through a baby monitor, they can hear everything in that room. A parent reading out a Social Security number over the phone. A family member sharing a bank account number. A sensitive work conversation. All of it audible to whoever has gained access — and baby monitors are among the most poorly secured devices in any home, routinely protected by nothing more than a manufacturer default password or a child's birthday.

For a nation-state intelligence service seeking to build a comprehensive intelligence picture of everyone responsible for defending critical infrastructure, this consumer convenience layer is an extraordinary gift. The Chief Information Security Officer of a major utility company has an Alexa in their kitchen. A senior grid operations manager has a Ring camera at their front door and a smart TV in their bedroom. A critical infrastructure maintenance contractor uses a home router that has not been updated in four years. And somewhere in that same home, a baby monitor is broadcasting audio continuously on a network secured with a four-digit password that has never been changed.

The Intelligence Preparation of the Battlefield: Before the attack begins, a patient adversary can use the compromised consumer IoT layer to map the daily schedule of every person responsible for defending the targeted systems. When they arrive at work. When they work from home. Who they speak with. What their home network looks like. What devices are connected to it. Whether their children's school is on the same network segment as their work VPN. A hacked baby monitor picks up a parent reading a Social Security number over the phone. A compromised smart TV records a conversation about work credentials. The attack vector is not a government facility with armed guards — it is a $60 device ordered from Amazon, set up in five minutes, and never secured again. The battlefield is already mapped. Most of the people defending critical infrastructure do not know it.

Section 07

The Preparedness Failure — What No One Has a Plan For

The most important question in this analysis is not whether a coordinated infrastructure attack is technically possible. It is confirmed possible. The question is: what happens to ordinary Americans during the hours and days before any organized government response reaches them — and does any realistic plan exist for that period?

The honest answer is no. There is no national civilian preparedness framework for simultaneous multi-infrastructure failure. FEMA manages natural disasters — hurricanes, earthquakes, floods — events that affect specific geographic areas for defined periods. Cyber Command manages military cyber operations. FBI investigates domestic criminal cyber incidents. DHS-CISA provides guidance to critical infrastructure operators. None of these agencies has clear unified authority over a simultaneous nationwide attack on civilian infrastructure, and none of them has a tested plan for communicating with civilians when the communications infrastructure required to reach those civilians has itself been attacked.

The Command Structure Problem

Martial law — the suspension of civilian government and transfer of authority to military command — has not been invoked in the continental United States since the Civil War era. The legal, logistical, and operational frameworks for implementing it at scale in a modern multi-state infrastructure crisis are untested. The military's own communications depend partially on civilian infrastructure. Mobilizing the National Guard to secure cities requires coordination infrastructure that may itself be compromised. The chain of command that would manage the response operates through systems that are targets of the attack.

The Question Nobody Has Answered: Consider a parent in Connecticut with two children at home and a mother in New York City who has no car and depends entirely on public transportation. Cellular is down. Television is static. Radio requires a device that most people no longer own, in a car that may not function if its onboard systems are disrupted. There is no way to know if the bridges are safe, if the subway is running, if it is safer to stay or to attempt reunion. There is no community gathering point with functioning communications. There is no neighborhood emergency coordinator. There is no plan. The Cold War civil defense infrastructure that would have provided answers to exactly these questions was dismantled after 1991 as irrelevant. It was not irrelevant. It was premature.

Section 08

Prevention Architecture and the Case for Civilian Resilience

Every problem described in this analysis has a solution. None of the solutions are new technologies — they are policy decisions, investment priorities, and architectural choices that are currently being deferred or deliberately reversed. The prevention framework operates at three levels: hardening the infrastructure itself, rebuilding backup systems that were dismantled, and restoring the civilian preparedness culture that was abandoned after the Cold War ended.

Infrastructure Hardening — The Non-Negotiables

1
Halt the POTS Decommissioning — Mandate Emergency Analog Preservation
The copper switching infrastructure already exists. The poles, the wiring, the central offices — built over more than a century — are already in place. The argument for decommissioning is commercial efficiency, not technical necessity. Federal policy should mandate preservation of analog POTS capability in all public facilities, hospitals, emergency services, and community centers regardless of carrier commercial incentives. The cost of maintaining this infrastructure is a fraction of the cost of a national emergency caused by its absence. If it is there and paid for, the question of why to remove it should require a higher burden of proof than quarterly earnings optimization.
CRITICAL — Communications Resilience
2
Mandatory OT Modernization with Federal Funding Mechanism
Legacy operational technology running on unpatched firmware from the 2000s in water treatment, power distribution, and transportation systems represents a direct threat to civilian survival. The federal government already mandates physical safety standards for this same infrastructure — minimum pipe pressures, chemical treatment levels, structural integrity requirements. Cybersecurity of that infrastructure must carry identical mandatory status. Existing infrastructure grants and critical infrastructure protection funds should be restructured to require OT security modernization as a condition of funding, with technical assistance for facilities that lack the expertise to implement it independently.
CRITICAL — OT Infrastructure
3
Security Clearances and Home Security Requirements for Critical Infrastructure Personnel
Anyone with privileged access to critical infrastructure systems — grid operators, water treatment supervisors, ATC controllers, telecommunications engineers — should undergo background investigation commensurate with their access level. This is standard practice in the defense industrial base under CMMC and personnel security frameworks. It is largely absent in civilian critical infrastructure. Additionally, given the surveillance inversion threat documented in this analysis, personnel in the most sensitive roles should receive guidance and support for securing their home networks and consumer IoT devices — because the intelligence preparation for an attack on the systems they protect may be occurring through devices in their living rooms.
HIGH — Personnel Security
4
EAS Hardening and Anti-Spoofing Authentication
The Emergency Alert System must be treated as a critical national security asset, not a collection of locally managed broadcast equipment running default passwords. Mandatory authentication for all EAS activations, cryptographic signing of alert messages to allow receivers to verify authenticity, centralized security monitoring of all EAS infrastructure, and mandatory patch management are baseline requirements. A system designed to reach every American during a national emergency that can be hijacked to issue false evacuation orders is not a resilience asset — it is an attack surface.
HIGH — Emergency Communications

Rebuilding Civilian Resilience — The Civil Defense Argument

The United States maintained a comprehensive civilian preparedness infrastructure throughout the Cold War. Neighborhood civil defense wardens. Community shelter systems. Public preparedness education. Emergency broadcast protocols that assumed the primary commercial communications network might be unavailable. All of this was deliberately dismantled after 1991 on the grounds that the Soviet threat had ended and the investment was no longer necessary.

The threat environment of 2026 is in some respects more complex than the Cold War. The Soviet Union had nuclear weapons but relatively limited ability to attack civilian infrastructure below the nuclear threshold. The current threat environment features nation-state actors with confirmed persistent access to civilian infrastructure, the ability to execute attacks that fall below the threshold of conventional military response, and the strategic motivation to exploit civilian vulnerability as a substitute for direct military confrontation.

Section 09

Conclusion — The Gap Between What Is Possible and What We Have Planned For

The scenario described in this report is not a distant hypothetical. Every technical capability required to execute it is confirmed in the hands of at least two nation-state adversaries. The infrastructure vulnerabilities that make it possible are documented, public, and currently unfixed. The pre-positioning — the patient embedding of persistent access inside US power, communications, and transportation infrastructure — is confirmed by the FBI, CISA, the NSA, and the Air Force cyber commander who called its potential activation "total war."

What is hypothetical is not the attack capability. What is hypothetical is the response. The United States has never tested its civilian response to simultaneous multi-infrastructure failure. The agencies responsible for components of the response have overlapping but uncoordinated mandates. The communications infrastructure designed to reach civilians during an emergency is itself a target — and in some cases already compromised. The analog backup systems that once provided resilience are being decommissioned for commercial reasons while the threats that made them valuable have grown more acute.

The analytical conclusion is not that this attack will happen. It is that the structural conditions that would make it catastrophic rather than merely severe are currently in place and trending in the wrong direction. The decommissioning of POTS. The absence of a unified civilian response authority. The dismantlement of Cold War civil defense infrastructure without a modern replacement. The consumer IoT surveillance layer that maps the homes and schedules of every person responsible for defending critical systems. The 73% of Americans who have no fallback communication capability if cellular fails.

The Question That Should Drive Policy: If cellular goes down tonight, how does a parent in Connecticut reach their mother in New York? How does a family know where to go, whether the roads are safe, whether help is coming, and how long they need to sustain themselves independently? The answer to that question today is: they cannot, they do not know, and they have no plan. That gap — between the sophistication of the threat and the absence of civilian preparedness — is not a technology problem. It is a policy decision. It can be fixed. It should have been fixed before this analysis needed to be written.
